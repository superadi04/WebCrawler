President Clinton is fighting hard for health care reform.  He is dedicated to guaranteeing health security for all Americans and to containing health care costs for families and businesses. Over 40 million Americans have no health insurance and millions more are just one illness away from losing it.

President Clinton will continue to work to make health care available to all Americans, improve the quality of health care, strengthen Medicare and expand coverage. The President is fighting for healthy families by:

Signing the Health Insurance Reform Act (Kassebaum-Kennedy Bill) which expands and protects access to health insurance by limiting exclusions for pre-existing conditions and allowing individuals to take their health insurance with them when they change or lose their jobs.
Enacting the Family and Medical Leave Act, enabling workers to take up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave to care for a family member without fear of losing their jobs.
Fighting to give all Americans quality health care and to reduce rising health care costs. Last year, medical care inflation had the smallest increase in nearly two decades.
Establishing a comprehensive Childhood Immunization Initiative to increase vaccinations and help ensure healthier futures for all children. In 1995, 75 percent of 2-year-old children were fully immunized, a record high.
Proposing a bold initiative to cut off children’s access to tobacco products and to reduce their appeal to children.
Fighting for full funding of the Women, Infants and Children Program (WIC) to expand nutrition services for mothers and their children.
Increasing funding for breast cancer research by 65%.
Making investment in AIDS programs a top priority: increasing funding for AIDS research, prevention and treatment by nearly 40%; and increasing funding for the Ryan White CARE Act by almost 112%.
Revoking the Reagan/Bush restrictions on abortion counseling (the “Gag Rule”), abortions in military hospitals and the “Mexico City” policy that banned distribution of family planning funds to overseas organizations that perform abortions or provide counseling on reproductive choice, even with private money.
Launching the Medicare Mammography Campaign to educate older women about the importance of detecting breast cancer early and about Medicare coverage of mammography services.
Enacting savings and structural changes that extended the life of the Medicare Hospital Insurance Trust Fund for an estimated three years without imposing new costs on Medicare beneficiaries.
Launching “Operation Restore Trust” to combat Medicare fraud and abuse. This new initiative has already paid $42.3 million in restitutions, fines, settlements and recovery of payments—a $10 return in recoveries for every dollar spent. Over 90% of this money is returned to the Medicare Trust Fund.
Launching Medicare Choices, a demonstration project that will give beneficiaries in eight cities and five rural areas expanded choices among different types of managed care plans and test new ways to pay for managed care.
Improving the quality of health care services provided to Medicare and Medicaid beneficiaries in managed care through a number of initiatives, including the establishment of the Foundation for Accountability, the Medicare Managed Care Quality Improvement Program and the Medicare/Medicaid Health Plan Employer Data and Information—all of which are designed to improve health care quality.
Working with states to test innovative approaches to Medicaid benefits and services, while preserving coverage guarantees for pregnant women, children, people with disabilities and older Americans. Over 650,000 previously uninsured people have received health care coverage under Medicaid demonstrations in eight states. When all 12 projects are implemented, 2.2 million people are expected to receive health coverage.
Developing a consumer information initiative to encourage Medicare and Medicaid beneficiaries to utilize preventive services, including mammograms and flu vaccines.
Proposing a $1.3 billion increase in veterans’ benefits—of which $1 billion will be directed to the VA health system to provide treatment for 43,000 more veterans.
Introducing regulatory reform efforts in the Department of Health and Human Services that will result in the elimination of 1,600 pages of department regulations, a 23% reduction.

Building on Our Progress

President Clinton remains firmly committed to guaranteeing health security to all Americans.

That is why he included reforms in his balanced budget proposal that would:

Make it easier for small businesses to buy and maintain affordable health insurance for their workers through the use of voluntary purchasing cooperatives.
Level the playing field for self-employed workers by increasing the self-employed tax deduction.
Help workers who temporarily lose their jobs keep health insurance by making them eligible for premium subsidies to pay for private insurance coverage for up to six months. This proposal would provide coverage for 3.8 million Americans each year.
Implement Medicare reform that further strengthens the Medicare Trust Fund beyond the reforms enacted in the President’s 1993 budget.
Expand the range of health plans available to Medicare beneficiaries to include new managed care options such as PSOs and PPOs that will compete on the basis of cost and quality and not by who can best attract the healthiest and wealthiest beneficiaries.
Take the first steps to providing long-term care by offering a new respite care benefit for families of those with Alzheimer’s disease.
Provide unprecedented flexibility for states to administer the Medicaid program, while preserving the guarantee of meaningful health benefits for millions with disabilities, pregnant women, poor children and older Americans in need of nursing home care.
Eliminate the requirement that states must seek and be granted a federal waiver to establish Medicaid-managed care programs.
Allow states to provide home- and community-based services without federal waivers.
