October 1, 1996

Dear Colleague:

This letter to you and other fellow scientists is prompted by our deep concern over what is at stake in the current presidential election campaign for the scientific community and our future capacity to serve the nation.

The Clinton/Gore Administration has established a clear record of support ing research, education, and the environment while greatly reducing the deficit. They have articulated the importance of science and technology investments for our nation's future where knowledge is the key resource. That commitment will be critical for sustaining the strength and vigor of our research enterprise while the difficult job of budget balancing is completed.

Many of us are deeply disappointed by Senator Dole's proposed economic plan, one that promises not only an unrealistic half-trillion dollar tax cut, but also an expanded defense budget that would cut deeply into the civilian budget. The effect that this plan would have on our public science and technology investments would be very serious.

Expert congressional budget analysts estimate that the Dole plan would re sult in a reduction of at least 40% in federal non-defense R&D. In fact, Mr. Dole's planned reductions are far greater than anything contemplated by even the most fervent budget cutters. Such reductions would devastate a national research infrastructure, which has taken 50 years to develop and is the envy of the world.

The Clinton/Gore Administration has worked with the U.S. science and en gineering community to enhance funding for basic research and to ensure that fundamental research remains unhindered by ideological pressures. They have ensured that the link between basic and applied research is sustained and are continuing to counter a sentiment on Capitol Hill that certain kinds of applied research are “corporate welfare.” This Administration has also opposed legislation to stymie funding for math and science education — proposals that would leave the nation at risk of losing a generation of bright, young researchers.

Those of us in the science and engineering community must not allow these attitudes and proposals to become the pattern for the next four years. Far too much is at stake to sit on the sidelines.

Some of you may be independent voters who have not felt comfortable within the structure of a political party. Some of you may have voted Republican or for a third party candidate in previous national elections. It doesn't matter. Some of us have felt the same way in previous campaigns.

We are not partisans in the traditional sense. We are, however, determined advocates for rational science and technology policies, and feel strongly that this leads us to support President Clinton and Vice President Gore in the 1996 election.

Moreover, we believe, as do President Clinton and Vice President Gore, that one of the more important roles for government is to represent the interest of the future to the present. We recognize that our national science and technology investments will be under pressure in the next years under any Administration, as it brings the budget into balance. We should also recall that the siren song of massive tax cuts and defense buildups is what ballooned the deficit in the first place.

In contrast, the Clinton/Gore Administration has demonstrated a commitment to the true pur pose of balancing the budget — namely, ensuring the foundation of further investment in our country's long-term economic growth and prosperity. Investments in research, technology, education, and the environment continue to be central to their vision and to their programs.

In a September 13, 1996, letter to America's Nobel Prize winners, President Clinton laid his views on the line clearly: “My Administration remains dedicated to nurturing science in America. This requires both maintaining federal investments in research and education and creating the economic climate that fuels private-sector investment, as well as private-public partnerships in science and technology. America's research colleges and universities are the bedrock of American leadership in science and technology . . . and research is and will remain a priority for the Administration.”

We look forward to the day when a bipartisan consensus on science, technology, and education will be restored and efforts, such as this one, will not be needed. Unfortunately, we have concluded that the differences are so sharp this year and of such important to the country that we must take our position to the public. We hope you also will reach that same conclusion and join “Scientists and Engineers for Clinton/Gore.”

You can make a difference on several levels. First, you can fax back the accompanying form and become a charter member. Second, we hope you also will support those candidates for federal and gubernatorial elections who share our rational views of the importance of science and technology to the nation. Third, we hope that many of you participating in the activities of “Scientists and Engineers for Clinton/Gore” will talk with each other. If you wish — and only if you wish — we will put you in touch with other members in your area, and provide suggestions for using these contacts to organize and participate in Clinton/Gore events or on a local or national basis.

We are asking you and your colleagues to join with us in this important endeavor by becoming a charter member of “Scientists and Engineers for Clinton/Gore” and by doing as much as feels right for you to help advance science and technology in the national interest.

With appreciation for your consideration,



Rita Colwell
Co-Chair		 Sally Ride
Co-Chair		 William Julius Wilson
Co-Chair
