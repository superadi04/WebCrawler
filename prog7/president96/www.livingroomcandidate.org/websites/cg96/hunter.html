Through partnerships with private landowners, state fish and game agencies, and local hunting and fishing organizations, the federal government has worked for decades to ensure healthy and abundant populations of waterfowl, game species and fisheries resources.   Building on this long-term, bipartisan effort, the Clinton Administration has strengthened conservation programs. Waterfowl populations and associated bag limits have been increased, in accordance with sound wildlife management practices and the continued implementation of the North American Waterfowl Management Plan. As a long-time waterfowl hunter, the President supports the rights of hunters to own guns and not a single hunter has lost a rifle or a shotgun as a result of the Brady Bill or assault weapons ban.

Supporting Regional Conservation Initiatives
The Clinton Administration has joined with state and local governments, conservation groups, and private landowners to protect and restore water quality and fish and wildlife habitat by:

Restoring the Everglades and Florida Bay through a comprehensive land acquisition program. The 1996 Farm Bill provides $200 million to acquire lands to protect and restore fish and wildlife, and the gives the authority to use $100 million worth of federal properties for land exchanges. The Preesident also signed the Water Resources Development Act of 1996 that includes his proposal to restore the Everglades.
Signing the Atlantic Coastal Fisheries Cooperative Management Act of 1993 to restore and protect near-shore fish species in the Atlantic Ocean, such as striped bass, sea trout, bluefish, and flounder.
Sponsoring the Appalachian Clean Streams Initiative to improve water quality and restore fish stocks in streams impacted by acid mine drainage. The President also approved funds to restore rivers and lands recently damaged by Hurricane Fran.
Continuing the progress on Great Lakes water quality by announcing a package of initiatives in Kalamazoo, Michigan, to accelerate the cleanup of Superfund sites, expand community right-to-know, and increase NOAA grants and technical assistance to states.
Restoring flood-damaged areas in the Midwest by signing emergency legislation to provide approximately $100 million to acquire conservation easements on flood-prone agricultural lands and to restore lands in flood-damaged areas.
Protecting valuable coastal wetlands by supporting reauthorization of the �Breaux-Johnston� Coastal Wetlands Planning, Protection, and Restoration Act to provide funding to protect and restore threatened coastal wetlands.
Providing the hunting and fishing communities a greater voice in the management of federal public lands by appointing representatives of the hunting and fishing organizations to serve on Resource Advisory Councils for Bureau of Land Management lands throughout the West.
Creating a National Monument to protect red rock canyonlands in southern Utah and to ensure continued opportunities for hunting, fishing, and outdoor recreation for future generations.
Working to protect trout streams from the hazards of mining by supporting comprehensive reform of the 1872 Mining Law.
Protecting the Wild and Scenic Clark�s Fork of the Yellowstone River by reaching an agreement with a mining company to avoid the harmful effects of mining.
Promoting forest health in the Pacific Northwest through the President�s Forest Plan. After the devastating flooding in the Northwest, he also signed legislation to provide increased funding to land management agencies to repair roads, culverts, fish and wildlife protective structures, and recreational facility sites in order to restore habitats and promote outdoor recreation.
Improving riparian habitat in western states through interim stream protections as part of PACFISH and through the development of a comprehensive plan for the management of lands in the Columbia River Basin.
Working to restore a world class salmon fishery on the Olympic Peninsula in the state of Washington State supporting the removal of two dams on the Elhwa River.
Protecting Kodiak Island and the Kenai River in Alaska. In a partnership with the state of Alaska, the Clinton Administration utilized portions of the $900 million Exxon Valdez Oil Spill settlement funds to protect over 400,000 acres of prime fish and wildlife habitat.

President Clinton has fought to protect the laws that help support America's conservation heritage by:

Promising to veto legislation that would have gutted the Clean Water Act, opened up wetlands to development, and left rivers and streams more vulnerable to increased pollution.
Vetoing Republican-sponsored budget cuts in the Environmental Protection Agency�s environmental enforcement and Clean Water programs.
Opposing a Congressional scheme to divest the American public of tens of millions of acres of federal public lands, an idea that would have had devastating impacts on hunter and angler access to public lands.
Alerting the American public to the dangers of Senator Dole�s reckless economic plan, which would require drastic 40 percent across-the-board cuts in programs that protect the environment, such as wildlife refuge operations, land acquisition, and federal fish and wildlife programs.
Opposing an anti-wetlands bill that would have opened up 60-75 percent of America�s wetlands to development. Senator Dole co-sponsored this same anti-wetlands legislation that would have accelerated wetlands loss.
Opposing industry-sponsored �grazing legislation� that would move away from the principles of multiple use of public lands toward dominant use for livestock grazing and that would limit public involvement in the management of public lands.
Opposing Republican efforts to cut longstanding, world-renowned federal fish and wildlife research programs, which are designed to combat wildlife diseases and to develop techniques for the sea lamprey, zebra mussels, and other nuisance species.
Fighting Republican-led efforts to mandate unsustainable clearcut logging, which would impact fish and wildlife habitat in the Tongass National Forest.
